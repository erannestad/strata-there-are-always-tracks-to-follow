Capital

Unearthed in this archeologic site in the Harvard Yard are currencies from across time. Old coins from the site stand-in for 17th century financial mechanisms used to mediate the transfer of indigenous land and resources (through trade, coercion, or theft) to the area’s colonists. The Massachusett, Wampanoag, and Nipmuck, among others were gradually alienated from their territory, thus also alienating them from the resources of the land, and consequently, the way of life that these resources provided them.

Today, state and corporate actors continue to employ financial mechanisms to colonize indigenous land. Alienated from their traditional territory and traditional way of life, indigenous groups have few other options but to comply with capitalist interests that seeks to exploit their labor, rights, and remaining territory. Whatever short-term wages, social impact investments, or project equity is offered to these indigenous groups in exchange for their compliance is offset by the further degradation of the land around them.

Such is the case of the Coastal Gaslink Pipeline project by TC Energy in British Columbia. This Canadian pipeline, like other such energy projects that seek to extract from within state-recognize land of indigenous groups, coerced struggling Indian Bands along the pipeline’s route allow exploitation of their territory in exchange for economic return in the form of temporary jobs and project equity.

From gold, bronze, and silver coins, to fiat currency of the modern day, these objects form a gradient of financialization that maps onto the mechanisms of the North American colonial project. Systems of land ownership and capital form the financial mechanisms and ownership structures that were, and are, employed to alienate indigenous people from the land that sustains them.

Narratives

The installation integrates a web-based augmented reality (webAR) experience of a swirling mass of archived objects from the dig site and contemporary artifacts. The artwork challenges traditional methods of historical interpretation and invites viewers to engage with layers of historical and contemporary artifacts.
